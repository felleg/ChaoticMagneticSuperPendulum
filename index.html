The Magnetic Pendulum

Physicists have always been fascinated by pendulums, and with good reason. They are very simple system that can be easily understood, yet still have applications in almost all fields of physics. While they might be simple masses at the end of a piece of string, modeling their movement has been used to solve many problems, from keeping time inside clocks to understanding quantum mechanics! This all changes, however, the moment a magnet is applied at the end of such pendulums. Indeed, this transforms an object that is otherwise completely understood into a concrete example of the butterfly effect. This effect can be summarized as "small causes can have large consequences". This idea ties into a much broader and complex concept of mathematics: chaos theory.
This simulation features the physics behind a magnetic pendulum. Notice how modifying any setting (even slightly) might result in changing the magnet over will the pendulum might end in an unpredictable manner. This is the butterfly effect! Try adding other magnets and changing the polarities to simulate the interesting motions of the pendulum.


Our Approximations

In order to simulate this experiment, the following approximations were made:

  • Treat the pendulum as a mass attached to a spring moving in a 2D plane. This is the small angle approximation: it mimicks closely the behavior of a pendulum with a very long string
  • Magnets are treated as magnetic monopole. While magnetic monopoles have never been observed experimentally in nature, they provide a great model to simulate magnetic forces because it removes the need to know the magnets' geometry to do computations.
